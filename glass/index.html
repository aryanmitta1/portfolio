01 — About GLASS

What GLASS is.

GLASS — Global Leaders and Scholars in STEM — is an honors program at the NYU Tandon School of Engineering that identifies and supports high-achieving undergraduates with the potential to become global leaders in innovation, technology, research, and entrepreneurship.

The program exists because the complex problems facing humankind — climate, health, security, energy, and communications — now play out on an unprecedented global scale. GLASS prepares engineers to meet those problems not only with technical depth, but with cross-cultural perspective, service, and leadership.

Scholars grow across five core competencies — the "windows" of GLASS — supported by a cohort seminar series, professional coaching, a required semester abroad, and annual funding for research, academics, and professional development. Each scholar connects their work to the Tandon Impact Areas, the UN Sustainable Development Goals, and the NAE Grand Challenges for Engineering.

02 — Mission Statement

Why I engineer.

"To help build the road to space — the launch, avionics, and sensing systems that make reaching orbit cheaper and safer — so that space becomes a tool for keeping Earth livable, not an escape from it."

I'm an Electrical & Computer Engineering student drawn to the space between code, circuits, and real-world flight. What pulls me toward a company like Blue Origin is the premise behind it — that the point of going to space is the benefit of Earth: using orbit, its resources, and its energy to take pressure off the planet we actually live on.

Through GLASS, my mission is to pair that technical work with the qualities the program cultivates: academic excellence, professional growth, a commitment to service, global competency, and leadership. I want to be an engineer whose work is technically excellent and pointed at that larger goal — building the infrastructure that lets space serve people back home.

03 — My Story

The path here.

I came to engineering through the specific problems, not a grand plan — I liked that a circuit either works or it doesn't, that code forces you to be precise, and that aerospace sits right where the two meet.

What keeps me in it is the stakes. The systems I want to work on — spacecraft, avionics, autonomous platforms — have to work far from anyone who can fix them, and getting that right means genuinely understanding what you've built instead of just getting it to run once. 04 — Global Impact

The goals I'm building toward.

GLASS asks every scholar to anchor their work to global priorities. Here are the ones that map to mine.

UN Sustainable Development Goal

SDG 11 — Sustainable Cities & Communities

Blue Origin's premise is that we go to space for the benefit of Earth — and that only holds if we stay good stewards of the environment we're expanding into. SDG 11 asks us to protect our shared natural and cultural heritage, and a dark night sky is part of that heritage for communities everywhere. My work modeling satellite brightness is aimed squarely at keeping orbit usable without dimming the sky people look up at.

  • Stewardship of the orbital & night-sky environment
  • Light-pollution & dark-sky protection
  • SDG 13 · Climate Action (supporting)
  • SDG 9 · Industry & Innovation (supporting)
NAE Grand Challenge for Engineering

Engineer the Tools of Scientific Discovery

The longer-term case for space is to move heavy, energy-intensive industry off Earth so the planet can recover — and every step toward that depends on cheaper, safer access to orbit and better instruments once we're there. My work on the Terawave constellation — modeling how bright each spacecraft appears from the ground — is that kind of tool: it lets us grow our presence in space while keeping the view of the universe clear for the science that comes next.

  • Cheaper, safer access to space
  • Satellite brightness modeling (BRDF)
  • Tools of scientific discovery
