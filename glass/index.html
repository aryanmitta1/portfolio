01 · About GLASS

What GLASS is

GLASS (Global Leaders and Scholars in STEM) is an honors program at the NYU Tandon School of Engineering that identifies and supports high-achieving undergraduates with the potential to become global leaders in innovation, technology, research, and entrepreneurship.

The program exists because the complex problems facing humankind, from climate, health, and security to energy and communications, now play out on an unprecedented global scale. GLASS prepares engineers to meet those problems not only with technical depth, but with cross-cultural perspective, service, and leadership.

Scholars grow across five core competencies, the “windows” of GLASS, supported by a cohort seminar series, professional coaching, a required semester abroad, and annual funding for research, academics, and professional development. Each scholar connects their work to the Tandon Impact Areas, the UN Sustainable Development Goals, and the NAE Grand Challenges for Engineering.

02 · Mission Statement

Mission Statement

“To use the precision of aerospace engineering to build more sustainable, livable cities: applying the modeling, sensing, and systems that fly spacecraft to the problems people face on the ground.”

I'm an Electrical & Computer Engineering student drawn to the space between code, circuits, and real-world flight, and the goal I keep coming back to is SDG 11, Sustainable Cities and Communities: making the places people live cleaner, safer, and better run. Working in aerospace taught me that the point of building anything for space is to improve life back on the ground, and that same logic is what draws me to cities.

The precision that goes into modeling a satellite or protecting a dark sky is the same precision a city needs to manage its light, energy, and environment. My aim is to carry that rigor from orbit down to the street, and through GLASS to pair it with the qualities the program cultivates: academic excellence, professional growth, a commitment to service, global competency, and leadership.

03 · My Story

The path here

I grew up between New Hampshire and Massachusetts — Nashua, Concord (for boarding school), Lexington, and Billerica — and I've been hooked on STEM for as long as I can remember. I'm based in Brooklyn now, but I've lived in Paris and spent time in 43 countries and counting, picking up a bit of the world along the way. I speak Kannada fluently, French proficiently, and some Hindi and Telugu.

That love of travel and learning about the world is the same curiosity that pulls me toward aerospace. It's the field I'm most excited about right now: it's growing fast, full of opportunity, and lets you work on things that have genuinely never been done before. Getting to build at that frontier is exactly what keeps me in it.

Outside of the work, I like to stay active with running, lifting, and hiking, and I follow and play a lot of sports. The rest of my time usually goes to cooking, baking, and good coffee, music, and exploring new places with friends.

04 · Global Impact

The goals I'm building toward

GLASS asks every scholar to anchor their work to global priorities. Here are the ones that map to mine.

UN Sustainable Development Goal

SDG 11 · Sustainable Cities & Communities

My primary goal is making the places people live cleaner, safer, and better run — and SDG 11 asks us to protect our shared natural and cultural heritage too, including the dark night sky that communities everywhere depend on. That's exactly where my aerospace work meets the goal: the same precision I use to model a satellite can model a city's light, energy, and environment, keeping both the streets below and the sky above in good shape.

  • Cleaner, better-run cities
  • Light-pollution & dark-sky protection
  • SDG 13 · Climate Action (supporting)
  • SDG 9 · Industry & Innovation (supporting)
NAE Grand Challenge for Engineering

Engineer the Tools of Scientific Discovery

Reaching a goal like SDG 11 depends on better instruments — the sensors and models that let us actually measure a city's environment or a satellite's impact on the sky. My work on the Terawave constellation, modeling how bright each spacecraft appears from the ground, is exactly that kind of tool: it lets us keep expanding what we do in space while keeping the sky clear for the science and communities that depend on it.

  • Sensing & instrumentation for Earth
  • Satellite brightness modeling (BRDF)
  • Tools of scientific discovery
